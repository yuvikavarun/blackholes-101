# 1. General Relativity (The Foundation)
In 1915, Albert Einstein proved that gravity isn't an invisible magnetic-like pull; it is the physical warping of the universe's geometry.
- **Spacetime:** Space (3 dimensions) and time (1 dimension) are not separate, they are woven together into a single, flexible fabric.
- **The Law of Motion:** Objects naturally travel in straight lines. However, massive objects (like stars) create "dents" in the spacetime fabric. A straight path across a dent naturally curves, creating what we experience as gravity and orbits.
- **Time Dilation:** The deeper you go into a gravity dent, the slower time passes for you compared to an observer safely far away.

# 2. General Relativity (The Foundation)
- **Formation:** They are born when massive stars run out of nuclear fuel. Without the outward push of nuclear fusion, the star's core violently collapses inward under its own crushing weight, while the outer layers explode (a supernova).
- **Spaghettification:** Gravity gets intensely stronger the closer you get to the center. If you fell feet-first, the pull on your feet would be millions of times stronger than on your head, stretching you into a long, thin string.

# 3. Finding and Proving Black Holes
- **Star Tracking:** We can't see the hole itself, but we can watch stars zipping in tight, incredibly fast orbits around seemingly empty patches of space (like the center of our Milky Way).
- **Gravitational Waves:** When two black holes collide, they send massive ripples through the fabric of spacetime, which we "listen" to using huge, sensitive detectors on Earth (LIGO).
- **Direct Proof:** In 2019, the Event Horizon Telescope captured the first actual photo of a black hole (M87), showing the glowing gas surrounding its dark silhouette.

# 4. The Role of Simulations
- **Why do we simulate in physics:** We cannot build stars in a lab or safely travel to them. We use supercomputers to crunch complex math (like Einstein's equations) to predict how extreme physics should work.
- **Why simulate black holes:** By simulating what a black hole should look like, we know exactly what to look for with our telescopes. When the 2019 photo perfectly matched decades of computer simulations, it proved our math and physics were correct.
